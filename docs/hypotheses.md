# Research Hypotheses

These are working hypotheses, not conclusions. Each one should be tested against evidence from multiple organizations and revised when the evidence does not support it.

## H1 — The bottleneck moves from execution to organizational systems

AI increases implementation capacity, especially in knowledge work and software development.

As execution becomes faster and cheaper, the limiting factors increasingly become:

- Prioritization
- Context availability
- Decision quality
- Cross-team coordination
- Review capacity
- Governance
- Leadership clarity

### What would support this hypothesis?

- Teams producing more output without improving business outcomes
- Increased review or coordination load after AI adoption
- Leaders redesigning planning and decision-making processes
- Companies reporting that clarity and context, rather than implementation, limit progress

### What would weaken it?

- Evidence that implementation remains the dominant constraint after broad AI adoption
- Organizations increasing output and outcomes proportionally without major operating-model changes

---

## H2 — The first durable AI transformation is organizational, not technological

Access to capable models is increasingly widespread. Sustainable advantage therefore depends less on access to AI and more on the organization's ability to integrate it into everyday work.

### What would support this hypothesis?

- Similar tools producing very different outcomes across companies
- AI adoption tied to management systems, incentives, hiring, and planning
- Companies redesigning workflows rather than merely deploying assistants

### What would weaken it?

- Durable advantage remaining concentrated primarily in proprietary model capability
- Tool deployment alone producing sustained organization-wide performance improvements

---

## H3 — Organizational design becomes a stronger differentiator than model selection

As frontier and near-frontier capabilities become broadly available, choosing the best model for each task remains useful but insufficient.

The differentiator becomes how effectively the organization combines:

- People
- AI agents and tools
- Proprietary context
- Decision rights
- Feedback loops
- Quality controls

### Research caution

This hypothesis may be premature in domains where proprietary models, data, latency, safety, or cost create a durable technical advantage.

---

## H4 — Managers evolve from task coordinators into context and system designers

When AI can execute more of the work, managers create less value by distributing tasks and tracking activity.

Their value shifts toward:

- Clarifying outcomes and constraints
- Designing workflows
- Making context accessible
- Establishing decision rights
- Building feedback and review systems
- Developing judgment across the team
- Combining human and AI capabilities

### Open question

Does this reduce the number of managers, increase their span of control, or simply change the work they perform?

---

## H5 — Prioritization becomes more valuable as execution becomes cheaper

Lower execution cost increases the number of ideas an organization can pursue. Without stronger prioritization, this may create more activity, fragmentation, and low-value output rather than more impact.

### Expected consequences

- Strategy must translate into clearer local decision rules.
- Product judgment becomes more important, not less.
- The cost of starting work falls while the cost of organizational distraction may rise.
- Stopping work becomes a core organizational capability.

---

## H6 — AI-native is an operating model, not a level of tool adoption

A company is not AI-native merely because employees use copilots or because it has deployed agents.

An AI-native organization changes its assumptions about:

- How work is decomposed
- How teams are staffed
- How context is shared
- How decisions are made
- How performance is evaluated
- How quality and accountability are maintained

This distinction should be tested across all case studies.
